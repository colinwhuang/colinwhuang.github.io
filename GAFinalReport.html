Abstract

      This project was created to design, build, and test a Foam Shell Flow Culling Station to automate the sorting and inspection of foam shell targets for inertial fusion energy (IFE) research at General Atomics, a leader in defense and energy innovation. This research is critical to expanding on the potential for efficiently harnessing fusion energy, a sustainable clean-energy alternative. Poly-alpha-methylstyrene (foam) shells, critical components in IFE, require precise tolerances in diameter, sphericity, wall thickness, and uniformity to ensure successful ignition under laser compression. With the growing demand for the production of IFE shells, it is necessary to automate the current manual inspection methods to save both time and money moving forward.

The final system, designed for shells ranging from 3–6 mm, operated in a fully submerged fluid environment to preserve the delicate shells. It comprised three key components: a converger to align shells into a single-file flow, a sensor-camera module to detect and simulate photographing shells for quality assessment , and a diverter to sort shells into pass or fail reservoirs based on operator input–(AI camera assessment was left out of the scope of our project). The system achieved an inspection frequency of 1 shell per 15 seconds and ensured safe handling of the shells.

Prototype testing confirmed the system's ability to effectively inspect and sort the foam shells while addressing challenges such as flow consistency, sensor accuracy, and mechanical robustness. The design demonstrated scalability, cost-efficiency, and the potential for seamless integration into IFE facilities.

This automated culling station enhanced research capabilities, significantly increased inspection throughput and supported the advancement of IFE as a clean, sustainable energy source. The project underscores the importance of engineering innovation in addressing global energy challenges while fulfilling professional and ethical responsibilities to promote planet sustainability and societal well-being.

Chapter 1: Project Description

Background 

General Atomics is a large defense and energy company, innovating in nuclear fusion + fission, military defense systems, and electromagnetic systems. Our team collaborated with Dr. Neil Alexander, who is the Director of Inertial Fusion Energy at General Atomics, to create a Foam Shell Flow Culling Station. Our project focuses on advancing the research and development of inertial fusion energy (IFE) technologies. Specifically, we are creating a cutting-edge system to sort and inspect thin walled (0.035mm thickness) Poly-alpha-methylstyrene foam shells used as targets in fusion reactions. Due to the nature of the material and their dimensions, the shells are extremely fragile and their safety is of utmost priority. These shells are critical for achieving the precise conditions needed for ignition under intense pressure and heat. If the dimensions on the shells are off even by a fraction, the shell will not ignite properly and the test data may be thrown out. Our goal is to design, build, and test a fluid-mechanical system that ensures seamless flow and sorting of shells based on their dimensional robustness, enhancing efficiency and quality control for future IFE power plants. This innovation aims to support the growing need for sustainable and clean energy solutions.

Review of Existing Design Solutions

Currently, there are no existing design solutions for this application, due to the fragile, low density nature of the shells and the requirement for the entire system to be underwater. However, there are many existing solutions to sorting balls which we drew inspiration from. Some examples of this are ball bearing diameter sorting mechanisms like the one manufactured by CDS manufacturing, as well as numerous food sorting mechanisms that showed how levers can be automated to divert the objects into different reservoirs. None of these examples dealt with fragile balls that floated in water, but their hopper design theory and diverting mechanisms were helpful designs to work off of.

Chapter 2: Final Design Solution

Figure 2.1: Final Design for the system excluding the camera

The system has 4 major components as seen in Figure 2.1: the converging mechanism (hopper), timing device (geneva drive), sensor and imaging window, and the diverter mechanism.  The system starts at the converging basin.

Step 1: Shells are to be poured into the converger, where they flow one-by-one into the timing device. To address shell clogging and the adhesion of the shells to the walls, an aquarium water pump maintains a water level within the converging basin with water within a separate reservoir.  Maintaining this water level within the converger creates a continuous flow within the system and ensures that the shells always remain submerged.

Step 2: The aligned shells pass through a spigot and onto the timing device.

Step 3: The timing device consists of a rotating circular plate with an inner ring of evenly spaced circular holes. The geometry and dimensions of the exit of the spigot and the rotating plate ensures that only one shell occupies each hole in the plate. A singular hole located beneath the plate is where each shell dispenses into the rest of the system. A DC motor with a gear reduction slowly rotates the plate such that the shells will fall at one shell every five seconds toward the inspection component of the system.

Step 4: The inspection section of the system comprises a sensor and camera window. The photoelectric sensor detects the incoming shell through the tube and the arduino calculates a delay to send a signal right when the shell will be passing through the center of the imaging window. Although taking a photo is out of the scope of our project, an LED flashes to simulate the automation of taking a picture.

Step 5: Positioned ahead of the sensor is the camera window, a rectangular segment of the tube with glass panes for precise and sharp imaging of the shells. Future AI software developed by General Atomics will enable the cameras to dictate whether a shell is good or bad. As of right now, diversion is achieved when the operator hits on a green (pass) or red (fail) button.

Step 6: Once the quality of the shell is determined at the camera window, the diverter will divert the flow of the system to the good or bad repository.  The diverter mechanism is a valve-like mechanism that turns 180 degrees both ways to sort the good from the bad shells and dump them in their respective sumps.  Below, Figure 2.2 shows the logic of controlling the servo motors. Because the shells need to be submerged in water at all times, the shells will be caught by mesh in the sumps and the excess water will flow into a final reservoir which will be pumped back into the system. Recycling the excess water back to the beginning of the system reduces waste.

        The function requirements of this component is to intake a large beaker of water and foam shells from an operator and to dispense out a single-file line of shells.   1. Electronics (Sensor/Camera/Power)

        The functional requirements of this component are to first sense when a shell is passing through the tubing, then tell the camera when to take the picture, and lastly tell the diverting mechanism which way to switch the flow (ie. pass → right, fail → left). The sensor will send HIGH voltage readings to the arduino whenever a shell passes, which can be utilized with an algorithm to calculate a time delay for when the “camera” (LED in our case) would take a photo. In our scope, the operator will push a button to divert the flow to the pass/fail sumps.

        The electrical system for our entire quality control mechanism is composed of a main switch that powers the entire system, engaging a pre-flush operation and servo reset operation. Then, a relay is used to power the pump in intervals by having a float switch. In addition, for testing the diverter there were good and bad sump buttons to divert the shells using the servo motor.

        The final design choice for the sensor was a fiber optic through beam photoelectric sensor, Keyence Fiber Unit FU-18. This device was chosen due to its performance when testing shells 3-6 mm in diameter. This type of sensor works by emitting a small IR beam through a detection region to a receiver across from it, and as an object passes through it refracts the light and changes the amount of light received. When the fluid flow inside of the tube was laminar, the sensor could detect significant changes in light intensity in all sizes of shells, indicating that we could then program our camera to take a photo at the correct time whenever the sensor receives a light intensity less than the set value. The code calculates the velocity of the shell by using the shell diameter and how long it was in the range of the sensor, which was used to calculate the time delay required to set off the camera. The amplifier connected to the sensor sends an NPN output signal to the Arduino when it is set off, allowing a relay to the other components in the system. The other design choices were too expensive for similar results, which also led us to moving forward with this component.

        The final design choice for the diverting mechanism was a simple rotating tube insert with slant, as seen below in Figure 3.3.1, due to the non-physical nature of flow diversion – ensuring safety of the shells. Chapter 4:‬ Prototype Performance

        

Component Performance Hypothesis

        In theory, the prototypes for the 3 components: the converging, electrical, and diverging components would perform very similarly to the final design due to the simplicity of the components and that the materials of the final design would be similar to those utilized in the prototypes.

        For the converging component, we predicted that the shells would stick to the sides of the hopper and clog, meaning that constant water flow or aggravation would be required. For the timing device, we predicted that it would be challenging for there to be continuous water flow with the rotating disk prototype, and that any shells that floated wouldn’t be separated effectively. For the other prototype with the separation of flow cones, we predicted that the timing may be too sporadic and may even not spread apart the shells enough to be tested, but that it would help the shells not get stuck.

        For the electrical component, we predicted that the sensor would have trouble seeing the shells if there was any turbulence or air bubbles in the flow. This theory was also extended to the camera, where we assumed that it would require precise laminar flow conditions to get a clear visual of the shell.

        For the diverging component, our theory was that the insert should effectively divert flow and that the magnetic rotation would be quick enough to account for how fast the shells would be moving. In theory, it should work effectively.

Test Conditions

                The test conditions of our experimental setup for the prototypes were as follows:

  • The flow conditions were constant after we impregnated the system with water and allowed the pumps to reach steady flow.
  • Once shells were inserted, we held the pump flow rate to 1 ft/s which matched the rate that the reservoirs released.
  • The shells we first used were polystyrene balls 4 mm in diameter, which are denser than the actual shells and sink in water. This allowed for the easiest test and preliminary proof of concept.
  • Later we tested with the actual shells, 3-4 mm  in diameter. These shells closely matched what the operator would be testing with GA.
  • The tests were airtight inside of the system, and open to the atmosphere at the insert and exit of flow.

Prototype Results        

Figure 4.1: Results of Converging Mechanism during first round of testing

                When testing the first component, the converging mechanism and timing device, our hopper clogged on average 50% of the shells if the shells differed from the designed size by more than 33%. If the shells were oversized, they wouldn’t fit through the hopper exit chamber, and if they were too small, they were susceptible to piling up and clogging that way. For example, if the design was intended for shells with an outer diameter (OD) of 6mm and 4mm shells were fed, clogging was imminent. However, inserting shells with similar dimensions to those designed for lead to much less clogging and more effective convergence. This led us to narrowing the scope of the project to only testing shells within a 10% dimensional variance to the largest size it was designed for. We built two separate hoppers for sizes 3-4mm and 5-6mm, along with their respective rotating plate sizes. Since they are all modular and attached to one another, they are easy to swap out for the operator.

Chapter 5: Design Recommendations and Conclusions

Future Recommendations

The current design only works for shells 3-4 mm in size. For future iterations, we recommend implementing additional size convergers to accommodate the larger shell dimensions.

If the product is to be mass produced, the final version will differ from the prototype made by having a swappable converger to accommodate different size shells. It will also have an input option to tell the Arduino how large the shells are for more accurate calculations.

There are no major safety considerations when this product is in use. Due to water flowing through the system, there is a need to watch out for any leaks or spills that may cause slip hazards or damaging of any nearby components.

To ensure that the hopper and timing device work as intended, keep the water level right above the hopper exit, as the shells are very sensitive to changes in pressure and can deform and get stuck.

Lessons Learned

        During the development of each major component of the project we gained some valuable insights about the mechanical design, fluid dynamics and automation for sorting the shells. One of the main takeaways was the necessity of keeping the shells fully submerged throughout the timing/separation process. Partial submersion led to inconsistent shell allocation, inconsistent flow behavior and structural damage to about 50% of all shells tested. By iteration of the timing design, we refined the system to ensure continuous submersion, which minimized defects on the shells and clogging issues.

        Another critical lesson was the importance of precision in the design of components. The timing device required multiple iterations in order to facilitate constant feed rate while preventing mechanical deformation of the shells. Initial designs resulted in frequent jamming, and major plastic deformation of the shells.

        Furthermore, sensor placement and selection was very crucial to the success of the project. Initial selection of sensors and testing showed sensitivity to air bubbles and turbulent flow, which lead to inconsistent detection of shells. By strategically positioning the fiber optic through-beam sensor such as where the least amount of air bubbles and more laminar flow is present in order to get more accurate results.

        Lastly, modularity is a key design consideration for our timing device. By developing interchangeable hoppers to accommodate different shell sizes, we enhanced the system’s adaptability. The only modification to be made for this accommodation is changing the hole sizes on the wheel component. This modular design approach allows for future modifications to be easy, making the system more versatile.

Conclusions

As we continue developing the Foam Shell Flow Culling Station, we have made significant progress in automating the sorting and inspection of foam shells for inertial fusion energy research. So far, we have integrated a converger, timing mechanism, sensor-camera module, and diverter, with early results showing the system can precisely handle ~10 shells. While this may seem small, future improvements to the converging mechanism could enable sorting much larger batches with minimal clogging. Due to the limited number of foam shells available for testing, our data currently supports efficiency up to 10 shells. To simulate larger batches, we tested 50-100 polystyrene shells, and the results suggest that as manufacturing rates increase, the system could perform reliably at higher volumes.

This project has shown the importance of automation in improving research and development. Automating the sorting process reduces reliance on manual labor, increases throughput, and enhances quality control. Moving forward, we will focus on optimising flow consistency, improving sensor performance, and exploring alternative sorting mechanisms to ensure shell safety.

Overall, this project has demonstrated the practical application of engineering innovation in addressing real-world challenges, in this case in clean energy research. Even though the system is still under development, our progress has shown promising results and potential in automating the inspection and sorting process of these foam shells; therefore, contributing to the advancement of inertial fusion energy technology. Future work will focus on completing testing, refining design components, and ensuring the system meets all performance and reliability requirements before the system is finalized.

Risk Reduction Effort

Incorrect Sorting

We tested the time it took for the diverter to switch utilizing arduino software. Through a 2x1 gear reduction, we increased the speed for a full 180 degree switch from 0.8 to 0.4 seconds.

We also tested the sensor to ensure that every shell that came through was detected. This made sure that for every shell that came through, the sensor could automate a camera to take the photo and also divert flow based on results. For our application, this was just an operator that sees the shell and diverts it as they see fit.

Fluid Leakage

In order to get rid of fluid leakage, we switched from an o-ring seal on the diverter to magnetic force, effectively getting rid of any physical contact point and area for potential leakage. We also added tubing barbs with tube fasteners to make sure fluid couldn’t leak out of our system.

Shell Jamming

In order to limit shell jamming, we tested several different hopper designs, both with and without fluid pressure. Through external research and test verification, it could be seen that an 80° hopper angle was the optimal exit valve and reduced clogging probability.

Varying spline profiles were also tested after the timing device for more uniform mass flow patterns so that the shells wouldn’t clog further down by the imaging window.

Sphere Structural Integrity

The safety of the shells is of utmost priority, so to ensure that none of the shells were damaged, broken, or deformed, our timing device and converger had to go through numerous design iterations. Originally, the timing device would roll the shells onto a hole in the 3d print, allowing them to fall through every 4 seconds. However, the shells had a tendency to be pulled under the 3d print and deform. Through experimental testing, we finalized our designs until every test recorded no damage to the shells. The shells were looked at under a microscope to ensure that there was no deformation.
